# knowledge-work — ground-truth

What to establish *before* writing. In this domain, ground-truth is the difference between a report and a plausible fabrication.

## Sources, in priority order

1. **Primary over summary.** The actual statute, filing, dataset, paper, or transcript — not a blog's paraphrase of it. If a claim rests on a document, open the document.
2. **Current over remembered.** Anything that changes over time — prices, office-holders, statistics, laws, "the latest X" — gets a fresh search and a dated source. Your training memory is not a source.
3. **The real brief over the assumed one.** Confirm audience, scope, and any required standard/jurisdiction before researching; the wrong scope wastes the whole run.

## Before writing, have in hand

- The primary source for each major claim you intend to make.
- The date of each time-sensitive fact, and confirmation it is inside the acceptable window.
- The brief/outline items the deliverable must cover, as a checklist.

## Red flags that you are NOT on ground-truth

- Citing a source you have not actually read the relevant passage of.
- A recent-looking source that merely *restates* an old figure without re-verifying it.
- An authoritative-sounding source you cannot trace to a primary origin (content farm, circular citation, the deliverable citing itself).
- Writing a claim first and "finding a citation for it" later.

When a claim cannot be grounded, that is a finding — flag it inline, do not launder it into confident prose.
